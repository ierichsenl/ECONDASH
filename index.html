GDP Growth & Inflation

Real GDP growth (bars) vs CPI inflation (line)
What it shows: Growth averaged 6.3% with only one negative year (2020). Inflation persistently elevated above 4%. What it means: The economy runs hot — growth above potential creates inflationary pressure. The 2025 combination of 6.65% growth + 3.28% inflation is the best ever, but fragile.

Twin Deficits

Fiscal balance and current account, % of GDP
What it shows: The fiscal deficit (red) has NEVER been positive. The current account (blue) mostly mirrors it. What it means: Twin deficit trap confirmed by S-I = CA identity. G3 needs foreign capital inflows every year. When investors panic, capital flight triggers currency crises.

GDP per Capita Convergence

PPP international dollars
What it shows: GDP/capita tripled from $3,100 to $10,378 PPP. Convergence is accelerating. What it means: Successful catch-up growth. At this pace, G3 could reach $15-18K by 2035. But the COVID dip shows how quickly gains reverse without structural reforms.

Executive Summary

Country G3 delivered exceptional growth averaging ~6.3% over 25 years, lifting GDP per capita from $3,100 to $10,378 (PPP). Poverty collapsed from 35% to 5.3%. However, growth masks persistent twin deficits — chronic fiscal deficit averaging −7.8% of GDP and recurring CA deficits.

Growth Cycles

Real GDP growth with average
What it shows: Three business cycles. Cycle 1 (2000-09): investment boom. Cycle 2 (2010-19): moderation. Cycle 3 (2020-25): deepest recession then strongest recovery. What it means: G3 is highly cyclical — large amplitude between peaks/troughs reflects absent automatic stabilizers and procyclical fiscal policy.

Savings vs Investment

% of GDP
What it shows: Investment exceeded savings 2004-2019, meaning G3 borrowed from abroad. Gap narrowed after 2012. What it means: When S < I, CA is in deficit by definition. The narrowing gap is concerning — it reflects falling investment, not rising savings. Policy challenge: raise savings via fiscal consolidation while maintaining investment.

Inflation Trajectory

CPI end-of-period
What it shows: Long-term downtrend but volatile. 2009 peak (12.6%) was supply shock, 2012 (10.5%) fiscal expansion. 2025 reading of 3.28% is lowest ever. What it means: Disinflation is real but fragile. Previous lows (2018: 2.5%) reversed sharply (2019: 6.7%). Structural deficit means any fiscal loosening reignites expectations.

Exchange Rate

Index 2000=1
What it shows: Steady 46% depreciation vs USD. Brief appreciation in 2007 during global credit boom. What it means: Depreciation rate (~2.5%/yr) matches inflation differential with the US, confirming purchasing power parity as the long-run driver. Direct consequence of fiscal dominance.

The Narrative

Cycle 1 (2000-2009): Investment-driven acceleration. Investment surged to 42% of GDP. Inflation peaked at 12.6% in 2009.

Cycle 2 (2010-2019): Moderation. Inflation oscillated 3-10%. 2018 equilibrium proved fleeting.

Cycle 3 (2020-2025): COVID shock (−5.78%) then V-recovery. 2025: best inflation ever (3.28%) but deficit still −7.1%.

Root Cause #1

Fiscal Dominance

Structural deficit NEVER better than −5.2% of GDP. Revenue (~20%) falls short of expenditure (~27%). Forces CB to accommodate.

Mechanism: chronic deficits → monetization pressure → inflation bias → depreciation → external vulnerability.

Structural Balance

Cyclically-adjusted — the smoking gun
What it shows: Every bar is negative, none better than -5.2%. This is the MOST IMPORTANT chart in the dashboard. What it means: Structural balance strips out cyclical effects. Always deeply negative proves the deficit is NOT caused by recessions — it is a permanent feature of fiscal policy. Even in boom years, spending exceeds revenue. Pure fiscal dominance.

Revenue vs Expenditure

The permanent shortfall
What it shows: Revenue grew from 17.7% to 20.2% of GDP. Expenditure stays around 27%. Gap never closes. What it means: Structural spending problem. Even at EM-average revenue (24%), deficit would still be ~3%. Both revenue increases AND expenditure cuts needed. Laffer Curve (Session 5) suggests room to raise rates.
Root Cause #2

Chronic Inflationary Bias

26-year average 5.9%. Sticky downward: barely below 3% even during COVID. Entrenched expectations. Recent 3.3% is encouraging but fragile.

Root Cause #3

Twin Deficits & External Vulnerability

S − I = CA: fiscal deficit absorbs savings → investment funded externally. Currency lost 46% vs USD. Vulnerable to sudden stops.

Twin Deficits Scatter

Fiscal vs CA, bubble = inflation
What it shows: Years plotted by fiscal (x) and CA (y) balance. Bubble = inflation. Most cluster in bottom-left (both negative). Bigger bubbles correlate with worse fiscal. What it means: Visualizes the twin deficit trap. Worse fiscal = worse external AND higher inflation. Three root causes are interconnected. Breaking it requires attacking the root: fiscal consolidation.

Verdict

Conditionally sustainable. Without fiscal consolidation, disinflation fragile, any shock triggers BoP crisis.

Pillar 1 — Detailed Measures

Fiscal Consolidation Program

The structural deficit of ~7% is the single most important problem. It drives inflation, currency weakness, and external vulnerability.

1A. Tax Base Broadening (+2pp revenue)

Revenue/GDP (~20%) below EM average (22-25%). Formalize informal sector via digital payments; progressive brackets for rising middle class; reduce exemptions.

+2pp GDP revenueCompliance costs

1B. Carbon Tax (Session 5: Pigovian)

Triple dividend: revenue (~0.5-1% GDP), deficit reduction, environmental correction. Revenue-neutral with rebates to low-income households.

Triple dividendRegressive without rebates

1C. Constitutional Fiscal Rule (Debt Brake)

Structural deficit ceiling of 2% of potential GDP with automatic expenditure cuts. Swiss model: reduced debt/GDP from 50% to 25%. The credible commitment device.

Permanent disciplineReduced crisis flexibility

1D. Expenditure Rationalization (−1.5pp)

Target spending from 27% to 25.5% via subsidy reform, public sector wage restraint linked to productivity, performance-based budgeting.

−1.5pp expenditurePolitical resistance
Pillar 2 — Detailed Measures

Monetary Credibility Architecture

The CB cannot control inflation while fiscally dominated. Institutional reform is the prerequisite.

2A. Central Bank Independence Act

Statutory price stability mandate; prohibition on direct government financing; fixed staggered terms; operational independence. Israel 1985 template.

Anchors expectationsRequires consensus

2B. Inflation Targeting (3% ± 1pp)

Published forecasts, press conferences, inflation reports. Maintain real rate ~3% until expectations converge. Taylor Rule neutral rate ~5.5% nominal.

TransparencyRigidity in supply shocks

2C. Financial Sector Deepening

Develop local-currency bond markets. Reduce "original sin" (FX borrowing). Build domestic investor base via pension fund development.

Reduces FX vulnerability5-10 year horizon

Laffer Curve Analysis: Session 5

Where Does G3 Sit on the Laffer Curve?

G3 operates in a narrow 17-22% band on the upward slope. Using EM-calibrated elasticity (e=2.5), the peak sits at ~28.6%. The critical insight: the Laffer curve shifts upward as GDP grows. The policy implication: base broadening shifts the peak rightward. Combined with a carbon tax (0.5-1% GDP), G3 can reach 24%.

Pigovian Taxation

Why a Carbon Tax Is Optimal for G3

Dividend 1 — Revenue (0.5-1% GDP). Dividend 2 — Externality correction. Dividend 3 — EOI catalyst. Canadian revenue-neutral model provides the template.

The Liz Truss Warning

What Happens When You Ignore the Laffer Curve

Markets do not accept unfunded fiscal expansion. G3 already runs a structural deficit of 7% — any signal of fiscal loosening would trigger a Truss-style market reaction. This is why the constitutional fiscal rule is essential.
